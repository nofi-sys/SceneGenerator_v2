The purpose of this scene is to establish the conflict between Blair and Skelton over the ownership of the horse Alabaster. The author also establishes the characters of Sylvia and Bulstrode in this scene.

The literary style of this scene is light-hearted and humorous. The tone is playful and the point of view is third person omniscient.

-Sylvia, old Tom, Mrs. Blair, and Skelton are sitting at a table talking. 
-The conversation turns to racing, and Skelton admires a colt of old Tom's. 
-This puts the black horse in the category of great horses, and Blair resolves to own him. 
-He will show old Tom the weakness of his conduct in letting himself be wrapped around Sylvia's little finger, and, if necessary, he will try his persuasive powers on Sylvia herself.
