The literary style of this scene is formal and polite. The tone is one of concern and worry, and the point of view is that of an omniscient narrator.

- Jenny is in her room writing a letter to her lover, Frederick Walcheren, after her father has forbidden her to see him again
- She tells him that she will be at the Bouchers that night and to meet her in the picture gallery after the fourth dance
- Jenny is excited and her cheeks are rosied
- She comes downstairs and her mother is in the hall
- Jenny is dressed in a light fawn tweed suit and her mother comments on how lovely and fresh she looks

The purpose of this scene is to establish the conflict between Jenny and her mother over Jenny's relationship with Frederick Walcheren. Jenny is determined to see Frederick, despite her mother's wishes, and her mother is determined to prevent her from doing so.
